Proposed Trajectory Comparison Method

JADON WAGSTAFF - May 3, 2017

Motivation

An important topic in ecology is yearly migratory trajectories of birds. Ecologists would like to know whether a birds trajectory diverges for fall and spring migration, and how much the trajectories diverge each year. They would also like to know how individual yearly migration divergences compare to trajectory divergences of other individuals or species. The advent of satellite tracking of small gps units attached to birds has made data collection of migration routs a reality. Ecologists are now able to gather large amounts of data relevant to the above questions; however, the methods currently used to analyze this data ignore a large portion of the data-points. Here I propose a method to compare migration trajectories through the use of a modified version of persistent homology.

Related Work

The current methods [2, 3] in comparing trajectories for bird migration is the following. A discrete number of latitudes are chosen along the migration trajectories, usually less than ten. For each latitude, the corresponding longitude is found along each of the trajectories. The longitude values are then used as a metric to compare two trajectories. Statistical methods such as ANOVA can then be used to answer questions about migration.

Contribution

There are some problems with the approach outlined above. First, migration trajectories are not always monotonic, if a bird makes a detour there may be two longitude values for a given latitude. Second, the longitude metric is not equivalent at two different latitudes. And third, only a small subset of the trajectory is considered for comparison.

To remedy these problems, a new method is proposed based on persistent homology. The method will calculate dimension one persistence with data from two trajectories, then the Wasserstein metric or the bottleneck metric could be used to compare trajectory pairs. To demonstrate the usefulness of this method, migration trajectory data for Cathartes aura (turkey vultures) [1] will be used to test the algorithm.

Proposed Method

In the proposed method, edges between sequential points in a trajectory are present at the start of the filtration, and the points in each trajectory corresponding to the wintering (breeding) areas are connected together. The result is a single connected component with one dimension-one homology group. A filtration is then made for this new structure; there is however, one constraint. In order to detect divergences, only edges between trajectories are allowed to be formed. The downside is that this may affect the theoretical guarantees about persistent homology. An algorithm was developed in C++ to calculate the filtration. The algorithm took advantage of the structure of the problem and ran in n^2 time with n space.

Results

The result of this algorithm is a persistence diagram showing divergences between two trajectories. Prominent divergences are marked by large differences in birth and death. These persistence diagrams can be compared using bottleneck distance or Wasserstein distance to compare different pairs of trajectories. For example, consider this paper [3] which compares consistency of long distance bird migration between two species. The Wasserstein distance could be used to compare all pairs of trajectories north and all pairs of trajectories south for one species. The mean distance and standard deviation of these values from one species could be compared to the mean and standard deviation of values obtained from another species. Conclusion

The algorithm proposed may be useful for Ecologists to utilize more of their data and detect patterns of interest in migration trajectories. More work is required to test the theoretical guarantees of the constraints on persistent homology, as well as proving the accuracy of the algorithm. After that, the method would need to be implemented on data where patterns have been recognized to test the validity of using persistence diagrams as a metric. Topological techniques seem to have merit for this type of data, and further investigation into the subject would be justified.
